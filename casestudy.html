Plant Health API

Project Overview

The Plant Health API is an early-stage digital tool designed to help gardeners and farmers monitor, diagnose, and improve plant health through data-driven insights. The API aims to connect data from multiple sources, including environmental sensors, user input, and image recognition models to provide actionable recommendations for common plant health issues such as pests, diseases, nutrient deficiencies, or environmental stress. Users can submit real-time data such as soil moisture, temperature, humidity, or leaf images, and the API will process this input and return information on the plant, plant disease, and potential treatments.

Needs Assessment and Materials List

There are a few skills that can help build an API tool. For this project, refer to the skills below.

    HTML, CSS, and Javascript Languages
    API Key - I obtained mine with Plant.id by signing up with an email for free!
    VS Code/Working Device: You will need somewhere to store your code and run your results.
    Working Wi-Fi and internet. The API will not work unless connected.
    Camera and photo upload so that the plant health can be processed.

Project Analysis

By integrating sensor data, user inputs, and image recognition, the API can help users identify plant health problems earlier and with more accuracy than traditional observation alone. This centralized system empowers home gardeners, small farmers, and even educators to monitor plant health dynamically, using accessible digital tools instead of relying solely on expert field visits.

Challenges and Solutions

    A primary challenge for the Plant Health API is achieving reliable accuracy with diverse plant species and conditions. Factors such as inconsistent photo quality, varied lighting, or incomplete sensor data can lead to misdiagnoses. Building a robust image recognition model requires training on thousands of plant images across different growth stages, climates, and disease types, which can be resource-intensive and time-consuming. Additionally, integrating third-party hardware like soil sensors or smart garden devices adds complexity, requiring clear data standards and secure connections to ensure user privacy and consistent performance.
    To address these challenges, the development team plans to start with a focused library of high-quality, verified plant images for common species and problems, expanding gradually through community contributions and partnerships with agricultural research groups. Basic rule-based checks can provide backup guidance when image confidence is low, ensuring users always get helpful feedback. For hardware integration, the API will use secure, standardized endpoints and clear documentation to make it easy for third-party devices to share reliable data. In the long term, combining user feedback with machine learning will refine the API’s accuracy, making it a powerful, practical tool for sustainable plant health management at any scale.

Results and Conclusion

    The early pilot of the Plant Health API showed promising results in helping users detect and address common plant health issues more quickly and confidently. During initial testing, the API successfully identified common leaf diseases such as powdery mildew and early blight. The API's recommendations were practical and easy to follow, making the tool helpful as a new farmer.
    The pilot results highlight the value of combining technology with accessible plant care. By integrating simple environmental sensors, user-submitted photos, and smart diagnostics into a single tool, the Plant Health API makes expert-level guidance available to anyone with a phone or basic monitoring device. As development continues, expanding the plant database, refining the image recognition model, and improving third-party device compatibility will help the API serve an even wider range of crops and growing conditions. Ultimately, a reliable Plant Health API could play a small but important role in promoting local food security, healthier gardens, and more resilient small-scale agriculture by giving people the knowledge they need to detect problems early and manage plant health sustainably.

At-Home Hydroponics

Project Overview

A Deep Water Culture (DWC) system is a simple yet highly effective hydroponic method that suspends plant roots directly in a nutrient-rich, oxygenated water solution. Unlike traditional soil gardening, DWC allows plants to absorb nutrients and oxygen more efficiently, leading to faster growth and higher yields — making it an excellent choice for growing heavy-feeding fruiting crops like tomatoes and cucumbers at home. With just a few key pieces of equipment and basic maintenance, an at-home DWC system can produce fresh, vine-ripened tomatoes and crisp cucumbers year-round, even in limited space.

Needs Assessment

There are a number of considerations for growing tomatoes and cucumbers using an at-home DWC system.

    Space & Location: You’ll need an area with enough room for the reservoir, plant canopy, and trellising. Ensure access to power for pumps and grow lights.
    Lighting: Make sure plants get at least 12-16 hours of direct light daily. This may vary across crops.
    Structural Support: Tomatoes and cucumbers are vining crops. You’ll need sturdy trellises to support the plants as they grow vertically.
    Water & Nutrient Management: A reliable water source for topping off the reservoir. A way to monitor and maintain water temperature, pH, and nutrient strength.Easy access to drain and clean the system.
    Air & Oxygenation: A quality air pump with stones sized for your reservoir.
    Time & Maintenance: Low-maintenance day-to-day but do require weekly nutrient checks, water level top-offs, regular monitoring for algae, root health, or pests.

Materials List

    Reservoir container: A large opaque storage tote or plastic bin. Must carry a minimum of the total gallons of all buckets combined.
    Buckets: 5 Gallons each for tomatoes and cucumbers. May vary on crop species.
        Growing medium: Expanded clay pebbles, rockwool cubes, or hydroton to support the plants in the buckets.
        Air pump: Sized for the volume of water. Many DWC systems will come with an air pump unless specified.
        Air stones: 1–2 medium or large air stones for good oxygen dispersion.
        Airline tubing & check valves: To connect pump to air stones.
        Hydroponic nutrients: Balanced nutrient solution suitable for fruiting plants.
        pH test kit or digital meter: To keep nutrient solution pH in the ideal range (5.5–6.5).
        Lighting: Full-spectrum LED grow light(s).

2D/3D Design

Project Analysis

Placed in a backyard, this system uses a sturdy, opaque reservoir fitted with buckets holding the plants in a floating or fixed lid. An electric air pump and air stones continuously aerate the nutrient solution, supplying oxygen to the submerged root systems. A simple trellis structure or vertical stakes supports the vining tomatoes and cucumbers as they grow upward, maximizing vertical space and sunlight exposure. Operating a DWC outdoors combines the advantages of abundant natural sunlight with the efficiency and water conservation benefits of hydroponics.

Compared to conventional soil gardening, this system reduces weeding, minimizes pest exposure in the soil, and uses nutrients more precisely. With basic regular maintenance, monitoring water levels, nutrient strength, and pH, the system can produce multiple harvests of fresh, healthy tomatoes and cucumbers right in the backyard.

Challenges and Solutions

    Challenge 1: Outdoor Temperature Fluctuations: Tomatoes and cucumbers thrive in warm conditions, but extreme heat can raise the reservoir’s water temperature, lowering oxygen levels and stressing the plants. Cold nights can have the opposite effect, slowing root activity.Place the reservoir in a shaded spot during hot months or insulate it with reflective material to reduce heat gain. In colder weather, use a small heater to maintain a stable water temperature. Monitoring with a simple water thermometer helps catch extremes early.
    Challenge 2: Pests & Outdoor Animals: Even though DWC avoids soil pests, backyard systems can attract insects, birds, or small animals curious about the plants or nutrient water. Use netting or fencing to protect the plants from larger animals. Inspect regularly for insects and treat infestations with gentle, non-toxic solutions suitable for edible crops. Keep the area clean and free of debris to discourage pests.

Results and Conclusion

    After building and operating the backyard Deep Water Culture (DWC) system for a full season, if there are strong yields from both tomatoes and cucumbers, on average, each tomato plant should produce 10–15 pounds of tomatoes over the season, while each cucumber vine yielded about 15–20 pounds. In this backyard setup, just 4 tomato plants and 4 cucumber plants produced a combined harvest of 100–140 pounds of fresh vegetables during the season. This amount can supplement the vegetable needs of a small family of 2–4 people, providing enough tomatoes and cucumbers for fresh salads, cooking, and preserving. Compared to a traditional backyard soil garden, the DWC system used water more efficiently, required less weeding, and delivered consistent harvests with fewer pest issues.
    The backyard DWC system demonstrated that with minimal space and proper setup, a home grower can produce a significant supply of fresh vegetables to support household nutrition. The system’s higher yields per plant and multiple harvests make it a great alternative to traditional gardening, especially in urban or suburban areas where soil quality, space, or water supply may be limited. Small-scale hydroponic projects like this can contribute meaningfully to local food security by providing a stable source of nutritious produce close to where people live. By reducing dependence on supply chains and minimizing food miles, such systems help communities build resilience against supply disruptions, rising food costs, or seasonal shortages.
